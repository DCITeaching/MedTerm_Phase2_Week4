The Integumentary System:
Your Skin as a Clinical Tool

The skin is the body's largest organ and first line of defense — and the most common site of injury in the ER. This reading covers skin anatomy, wound classification, burn staging, and the clinical vocabulary you need to document every integumentary encounter.

What
Identify all 3 skin layers, classify wounds and burns, apply ABCDE screening
Why
ER clinicians assess skin integrity in every single patient encounter — from minor scrapes to full-thickness burns

Burn Staging: Four Degrees of Severity

Burn classification is one of the most tested skills on the Rotation A Boards because the clinical signs are very specific and the treatment decisions depend entirely on accurate staging. The key differentiators are: depth of tissue involvement, presence or absence of blisters, skin appearance, and pain level.

When a 3rd or 4th degree burn patient reports no pain at the burn site, this is because the nerve endings in the dermis have been completely destroyed. The surrounding unburned tissue, however, is intensely painful because those nerve endings are intact and responding to the heat exposure. This apparent paradox — no pain at the worst burn site — is a critical clinical finding.

TBSA drives IV fluid resuscitation orders and determines whether ICU admission is required.

ABCDE Melanoma Screening & Pressure Ulcer Staging

ABCDE: The Melanoma Screening Tool

Melanomamelan/o (dark/black) + -oma (tumor)Malignant tumor of melanocytes. Most dangerous form of skin cancer — highly metastatic if caught late. is the most dangerous form of skin cancer, arising from melanocytesmelan/o (dark) + -cyte (cell)Pigment-producing cells in the epidermis. Source of melanoma tumors.. The ABCDE mnemonic gives you a systematic assessment tool for any suspicious skin lesion:

  • A — Asymmetry: One half of the lesion does not match the other. Benign moles are symmetrical.
  • B — Border: Irregular, ragged, notched, or blurred edges. Benign moles have smooth, even borders.
  • C — Color: Multiple shades (brown, black, red, white, or blue) within one lesion.
  • D — Diameter: Greater than 6mm (pencil eraser size) — though melanomas can be smaller when first detected.
  • E — Evolution: Any change in size, shape, color, or new symptoms (bleeding, crusting, itching) over time.

Document your ABCDE findings fully: "9mm lesion, left shoulder. Asymmetric. Borders irregular with satellite lesion noted. Variegated pigmentation: brown, black, and focal red area. Evolution: patient reports lesion has darkened over 3 months. Dermatology referral placed."

Pressure Ulcer Staging (I–IV)

Pressure ulcers form when sustained pressure reduces blood flow to tissue, typically over bony prominences (sacrum, heels, occiput). Staging is determined by the depth of tissue involvement:

  • Stage I: Intact skin with non-blanchable redness. Skin appears darker in pigmented patients.
  • Stage II: Partial thickness loss — shallow open wound with pink/red bed, or intact blister.
  • Stage III: Full thickness loss — subcutaneous fat visible. No bone, tendon, or muscle exposed.
  • Stage IV: Full thickness with bone, tendon, or muscle exposed. Slough or eschar often present.
Staging RuleThe presence of necrosisnecr/o (death) + -osis (condition)Death of body tissue from inadequate blood supply or severe injury. Must be removed for healing. or eschar means you cannot accurately stage a pressure ulcer — it's documented as "unstageable" until the wound is debrided and the base can be assessed.

Task 1 · Burn Classification
Classify the burn on Marcus's forearm AND on his palm separately. Use clinical criteria — don't just name the degree. Explain what evidence from the scenario supports each classification.
Forearm: Look for the presence of blisters + moist wound bed. Palm: The skin is dry with no blisters — which layer is involved? Remember: the degree is determined by depth of injury, not by pain level alone.
Task 2 · TBSA Calculation
Using the Rule of Nines, estimate the TBSA for Marcus's burns. Show your calculation. Note: the forearm is approximately 1/2 of the arm's total 9%, and the palm is approximately 1% TBSA.
Each entire arm = 9%. The forearm alone is roughly half of that = 4.5%. The palm = approximately 1%. Total TBSA = approximately 5.5%. This matters because TBSA >20% typically requires IV fluid resuscitation — Marcus is below that threshold but still needs monitoring.
Task 3 · Wound Healing Prediction
Predict what the wound healing process will look like for Marcus's forearm burn over the next 3 weeks. Name each healing phase, the expected clinical signs during that phase, and what you would document in the EHR at each follow-up.
Phase 1 should show erythema and edema expanding slightly before decreasing. Phase 2 — watch for granulation tissue developing in the wound bed (beefy-red, moist). Phase 3 — the wound contracts and scar tissue forms. If you see eschar or purulence at any point, that signals a complication requiring debridement or antibiotics.
Task 4 · SOAP Note — Objective Section
Write the Objective (O) section of Marcus's SOAP note. Include all vitals provided, a complete wound description using correct clinical terminology, and any additional physical examination findings implied by the scenario. Use at least 6 Week 4 terms correctly.
Key terms to include: erythema (surrounding tissue), blisters/blistering, moist wound bed, TBSA, the burn degrees for each area, ecchymosis (if any bruising noted), dermis/epidermis in your description of depth. Format: state the facts, not your interpretation — that goes in Assessment (A).
